# Speech and talk delivery rules

Distilled from Glenn Kramon's *Winning Writing* Session 8 ("Wowing the Crowd"), Stanford GSB. The rules a writing class teaches about spoken delivery, because most "bad speeches" are actually badly *written* speeches read aloud.

## The shape of a talk

A speech is not an essay. The reader of an essay can reread a sentence; the listener of a speech cannot. So:

- **The end matters more than the beginning.** In an essay you front-load the thesis (BLUF). In a speech the last thing you say is the line they leave the room repeating. Write the closing first; everything else builds toward it.
- **Repeat the main thought.** State the thesis in one sentence early. Then repeat it — once in the middle, once at the close. The audience will only remember a single sentence; you choose which one.
- **Keep it short.** A 20-minute talk that lands beats a 45-minute talk that drifts. If you cannot say it in 20 minutes, you do not understand it yet.
- **Keep it simple.** No nested clauses. No jargon. Every sentence parses on first hearing or it is wasted.
- **Vary sentence length** — the same rule as in writing, but the ear is even less forgiving than the eye. A long sentence after three short ones lands. Three long ones in a row puts the room to sleep.

## The 12 rules of wowing the crowd (Kramon, Session 8)

1. **Remember your audience.** What will make *them* laugh, cry, sigh? Not "what do I want to say" — "what do they need to hear."
2. **Open with humor, ideally self-deprecating.** Earns the room's permission to keep listening. A laugh in the first 60 seconds is worth more than a clever sentence in minute 12.
3. **Ask a question, then ask for a show of hands.** *"How many of you have ever…?"* Converts a passive audience into a participating one. They are now in the talk, not watching it.
4. **State your main thought in a sentence early.** One sentence, no qualifications. Say it within the first two minutes. Repeat it later.
5. **Use that one thought to guide the writing.** Every story, every quote, every joke earns its place by serving the main thought or it gets cut.
6. **Tell a story — and make sure it makes your point.** Stories are 12× more memorable than statistics alone. But a story that does not connect to the thesis is a digression that costs you the room.
7. **Use inspiring quotes.** One or two land harder than your own paraphrase of the same idea. Attribute precisely; misattribution kills credibility.
8. **Be upbeat and uplifting.** Even when the subject is hard. Audiences do not return calls from people who leave them feeling worse.
9. **Keep it simple.** No nested clauses, no jargon, no acronyms you have not defined. The listener cannot rewind.
10. **Keep it short.** Cut your draft by a third. Then read it aloud and cut again. The clock is the most honest editor you will ever have.
11. **Repeat important words and thoughts.** What you repeat is what they remember. Choose deliberately. Three repetitions of the same phrase across a talk is intentional; five is a tic.
12. **The end matters more than the beginning.** In speeches, the closing line is the one they quote. Write it first. Memorize it. Land it without notes.

## How to draft a talk

Order of operations (do NOT start at the top):

1. **Write the closing line.** One sentence. The line you want quoted. If you cannot write it, you do not have a talk yet.
2. **Write the main thought.** One sentence. Different from the closing — this is the *argument*; the closing is the *image*.
3. **Pick the story.** One story that proves the main thought. Real people, real names, one scene. If you have more than one story, you have more than one talk — pick a lane.
4. **Pick the opener.** Self-deprecating humor or a question + show of hands. Whichever earns the laugh or the raised hand faster, given this audience.
5. **Outline the arc.** Opener → main thought → story → second beat of the story or a quote → restate the main thought → closing line.
6. **Draft the body.** Short paragraphs, the way you actually speak.
7. **Cut by one third.** Every talk gets longer than planned. Pre-cut so the live version still fits.
8. **Read aloud at performance pace.** Mark every place you stumbled. Stumbles are the listener's confusion points; rewrite them.

## What never goes in a talk

- **Slides full of text.** If the audience is reading, they are not listening. Slides are for one image, one number, or one sentence — never bullets.
- **A list of bullet points read aloud.** This is the single most common bad-talk failure mode. The list says "I did not have time to write this in prose."
- **Quotes longer than two sentences.** Edit ruthlessly. The room cannot hold a five-sentence quote in working memory.
- **Acronyms you have not defined on first use.** Even one unfamiliar acronym makes the listener stop tracking the next sentence.
- **A long thank-you / credits section at the top.** Land the opener first. Acknowledgements at the start are throat-clearing.
- **Apologies.** *"I'm sorry I'm a bit underprepared,"* *"this is going to be quick"* — never. Open with the work, not the meta-commentary.
- **Ending on a process note.** *"And with that, I'll take questions"* is not an ending. End on the closing line. Then take questions.

## Reading a room

Watch the audience while you talk:

- **Heads up = engaged.** Keep going.
- **Heads down (looking at phones) = lost them.** Either compress hard or land a story now.
- **Laughter in the wrong place = nervous, not amused.** Slow down. The next sentence needs to be plain and warm.
- **Silent on a line you expected to land = the line did not work.** Do not repeat it. Move on. Cut it for next time.
- **A single laugh from the back = you can repeat the joke later** in a callback. Earned.

The Andrea Elliott rule (Pulitzer-winning author, in conversation with Kramon): *"When you tell a story, watch your audience's reaction. Modify it accordingly for the next time."* Every talk is a draft of the next talk.

## The opening — three workable archetypes

| Archetype | When it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-deprecating story** | Always safe. Establishes humility. | *"I was the worst-prepared student in this class four years ago. So Glenn invited me back as a guest."* |
| **Show-of-hands question** | When the topic is something the audience has lived | *"How many of you have ever opened an email and immediately wished you hadn't sent it?"* |
| **A single concrete image** | When the topic is something most people have NOT lived | *"In a refugee camp in southern Uganda, an eight-year-old girl I met last month walked four miles before sunrise to get to a class her teacher no longer teaches."* |

Never open with: *"Thank you for having me,"* *"I'm honored to be here,"* or *"Let me start by saying…"* — every one of these is throat-clearing.

## The closing — three workable archetypes

| Archetype | When it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Callback to the opener** | Almost always. Bookends the talk. | If you opened with the eight-year-old: *"Tomorrow morning she'll walk four miles again. We can be the reason her teacher is still there when she arrives, or we can be the reason no one is."* |
| **A single quote** | When someone said it better than you can | *"As Maya Angelou said: 'People will forget what you said. People will forget what you did. But people will never forget how you made them feel.' Tonight, I hope you feel something."* |
| **A specific ask** | For a fundraiser, a recruiting talk, or any call to action | *"There are two ways to leave this room. With your hands in your pockets. Or with one of them holding a pen, signing the sheet at the back."* |

## Speaking to a big room vs. a small one

- **Under 25 people:** conversational. Sit if it fits the room. Eye contact with individuals. Pauses are louder than projection.
- **25–100 people:** stand. Project. Slower delivery — words drift further than you think. Pause longer between paragraphs.
- **100+ people:** the show-of-hands trick stops working past ~200 (you cannot count). Substitute with a polling question to the front row, repeated for the room. Volume up, pace down further, gestures bigger.
- **5,000+ people:** TED-talk discipline. Memorize the first 30 seconds and the last 30 seconds word for word. Nothing else. The middle improvises around the prepared bookends.

## The pre-talk checklist

- [ ] **Closing line written and memorized** — verbatim, deliverable without notes.
- [ ] **Main thought in one sentence** — said early, repeated later.
- [ ] **One story** — not three. One.
- [ ] **Opener tested aloud** — self-deprecating or audience-engaging.
- [ ] **No bullet slides** — every slide is one image, one number, or one sentence.
- [ ] **Cut by one-third from the first draft** — and pre-cut another 10% for the live overrun.
- [ ] **Read aloud at performance pace** at least twice, with a timer.
- [ ] **Acronyms defined** on first use.
- [ ] **Quotes attributed correctly** — Maya Angelou said it, not Mark Twain.
- [ ] **Final 30 seconds memorized cold** — no glancing at notes for the close.

## The test

Would one person in the audience repeat your closing line to someone else tomorrow morning? If yes, you wrote a talk. If no, you wrote a memo and read it aloud.
