# Performance review rules

Distilled from Glenn Kramon's *Winning Writing* (Stanford GSB), built from 30 years of writing reviews at the New York Times. The review is one of the highest-leverage documents you'll ever write about someone, because it sits in the file forever and your staff is watching how you handle it.

## Philosophy

A review is **a letter to one person**, not a report card sent up to management. Read aloud, it should sound like you talking to them. If it sounds like it's written to their mother (*"Glenn should strive to shorten his sentences..."*), rewrite it.

Your staff is watching how you review the weakest performer more than anyone else. They are deciding two things:
- *Is she on my side, or against me?*
- *If I were in trouble, would she fight for me?*

If they decide you are against them, they will route around you. The review either earns you their trust or burns it.

## The seven rules

### 1. Write it as a letter TO the person, not ABOUT them
*"You ably conceived and guided our teams,"* not *"Peter ably conceived..."*. The third-person voice is the kindergarten-report-card voice. Reviewers hate it. Direct address is conversational and respectful, and forces you to remember you are talking to a human.

### 2. Lead with what you LIKE, then what you WOULD LIKE
Same principle as op-ed feedback: open with praise, then constructive direction. Never lead with the negative. People are wired to weight a single negative interaction six times more strongly than a positive one. Some research says you need five positives for every negative just to keep the relationship neutral; in practice closer to **twenty to one**.

Use *"what I would like"* phrasing, not *"what I dislike"* or *"what you should stop doing."*

### 3. Specify near-term AND long-term goals
- **Most significant accomplishments of the past period** — never forget the big ones. Ask the person to remind you in their self-review so you cannot miss them.
- **Goals for the current year** — specific, named, with what you will look for.
- **Goals for the years beyond, including their next job** — this is the rule that makes them love you forever. Most bosses care only about their employee's performance in the current role. A boss who names what the employee needs to do to get to *their next role* signals that they care about the person, not just the output. If the person says *"but I'd be working for someone else by then,"* answer *"I want you to succeed regardless."*

### 4. Incorporate feedback from colleagues
The staff is afraid this is snitching. It isn't. Ask the colleagues two things only:
- *"What do you like about Glenn?"*
- *"What could Glenn do even better?"*

Refuse to take *"what I dislike about Glenn."* If they give it anyway, do not pass it along. Constructive only.

Mentioning colleague feedback in the review is what makes it stick. *"Your colleagues praise you for X"* lands ten times harder than *"I think you're good at X."* The employee writes off the boss as obligated to compliment; they cannot write off the peers.

### 5. Use vivid, specific examples
Vague reviews are the worst kind. *"Articles can be too long and boring"* is an insult with no fix. **Never call someone's work boring.** The B-word kills the rest of the review — they stop reading.

Instead: *"While many of your articles sang, the X piece on Y could have been shorter and punchier. It needed more sense of place and people, and at least one more named voice."* Now they know what to do tomorrow.

### 6. Never psychoanalyze, never ambush
Two distinct anti-patterns, both fatal:

**Psychoanalyzing** — never speculate about *why* the person had a bad year. Do not mention divorces, illnesses, family troubles, depression, burnout in the review. *"We understand your performance was affected by your divorce and the death of your father"* is humiliating, and the bosses reading the review now have those details in the personnel file forever. If you genuinely want to help, have a separate conversation: *"It's been a tough year. Tell me what I can do."* That is private. The review focuses only on behavior and the work.

**Ambushing** — never put bad news in the review that you have not already discussed with the person. *"Your confrontation with the other editor six months ago cannot be tolerated"* is an ambush if you never raised it at the time. The employee will say *"how dare you save this for the review,"* and they will be right. Discuss every negative item *before* it appears in writing.

### 7. End with thank you
The two most important words in any review are *thank you*. Many of your people could be earning more somewhere else, working easier hours. They are choosing to be here. The thank-you in a review carries weight that a thank-you at four in the morning after a deadline does not, because it is in writing and elaborate.

The test: would they save the review if their house were on fire? If yes, you did it right.

## Structure cheat sheet

```
Dear [Name],

[Paragraph 1 — what you like]
We couldn't have done X without you. You [specific verb] Y.
Your colleagues describe you as [3-5 specific adjectives, citing them, not you].

[Paragraph 2 — what you would like]
We think you can do even better. [Specific behavior or skill],
especially [vivid example with what good looks like].

[Paragraph 3 — acknowledging their self-review]
You mentioned [goal from their self-review] as a goal. We've noticed
[evidence of progress]. Keep [specific behavior]; cut [specific behavior].

[Paragraph 4 — the next job]
We want you to know you have a great future here. To get to [the role
they named, or one you think fits], focus on [2-3 specific competencies].

[Paragraph 5 — thank you]
Thank you for [specific contribution]. You could be earning more
somewhere else. We're lucky you chose us.

[Sign-off]
```

## Self-review: what to put in it

If you are writing your own, the same three buckets:
1. Most significant accomplishments — be specific, named projects, real numbers.
2. Goals for the current year — what you will deliver, by when.
3. Ambitions for the years beyond, including the job you'd want next — give your boss something to react to.

## Reviewing the weakest performer

The hardest review you'll write. Your staff is watching this one most. Do all of the above PLUS:

- **Schedule the follow-up explicitly.** *"Let's plan to meet every other Monday to review your progress."* The review is not a once-a-year ambush; it is the kickoff of a managed sequence.
- **Be honest about what's at stake.** *"Unless we see improvement on [specific behaviors], we may need to find you a different role."* This sounds harsh; it is actually the kindness. People who later get managed out and were never warned in writing are right to be furious.
- **Name the behaviors, not the person.** *"Colleagues say you should stop blaming others and help solve problems yourself"* — behavior. Not *"you are a blamer"* — character.
- **Show you are trying to help.** *"Here's what I'll do to support you: weekly check-ins, an introduction to [person who can mentor], a clear scope reduction for the next sprint."*

The angels on your shoulder are the colleagues who want this person to improve. Write to them too, by writing the review the colleagues would consider fair.

## Anti-pattern catalog

| Anti-pattern | What it looks like | What to do instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Kindergarten-report-card voice** | *"Glenn should strive to..."* | *"Strive to..."* (write TO the person) |
| **Passive-aggressive credit** | *"Has been actively involved on our team that won four Pulitzers."* | *"We couldn't have won those four Pulitzers without you. You ably conceived and guided..."* |
| **The B-word** | *"Your articles can be too long and boring."* | Never *boring*. Name the specific piece, specific length issue, specific fix. |
| **Psychoanalysis** | *"Your divorce affected your performance."* | Cut it. Have a private conversation. The review focuses on behavior only. |
| **Ambush** | Bad news in the review that was never raised before. | Discuss every negative item before it appears in writing. |
| **Vague compliments** | *"Will continue the evolution in his own way so that the newspaper is provocative, informative, important."* | Name specific things they did; name specific things you want them to do. |
| **Self-centered framing** | *"I'd like to thank myself for my leadership. Some of you did useful things too."* (Colbert) | Make them feel like *they* are the cleverest person in the room. Compliment them, not yourself. |
| **No long-term horizon** | Only this year, only this job. | Always include where you want them to be in five years, including their next role. |
| **Closing with a process note** | *"I look forward to next year's review."* | Close with thank you, specifically. |

## Reframing the common blunt feedback line

Same principle: focus on the WORK, not the person. Say what you LIKE and what you WOULD LIKE. Ask a question instead of declaring.

| Blunt | Better |
|---|---|
| *"You don't smile enough."* | *"I noticed in client meetings, your enthusiasm for the subject doesn't always show in your face. Worth thinking about whether the room can read what you feel."* |
| *"You're intellectually intimidating."* | *"We all know how smart you are. You have more influence when you add a little self-deprecating humor — it makes you accessible."* |
| *"Stop cutting off your colleagues."* | *"I love a lot of the ideas you share. Give the room more space to get there too."* |
| *"You're not speaking up enough."* | *"In the moments you've contributed, we've all benefited. I'd love to see more."* |
| *"You're too blunt."* | *"I appreciate your candor. To borrow from Mary Poppins, a spoonful of sugar helps the medicine go down."* |
| *"Stop asking so many questions."* | *"I hired you because you're smart. I trust you to figure more things out on your own."* |
| *"I'm sick of you being late on deliverables."* | *"I'd rather you be on time at 90% than late at 100%. Can we talk about how to meet our deadlines?"* |
| *"You're burning out."* | *"I'm concerned about whether you can sustain this pace. Is it time to take a break?"* |

The pattern: name what you LIKE first, then what you WOULD LIKE, focused on the work, ideally phrased as a question.

## The house-on-fire test

Write the review so that if the person's house were on fire, they would grab the printout along with their kids, the dog, and the passports. That is the bar for your star performers. For your weakest, write it so that even on the day you have to manage them out, they say *"you were always fair."* Both standards are within reach. Neither is automatic.
