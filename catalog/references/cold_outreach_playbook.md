Appendix: Cold Outreach Playbook
From Rachel Konrad's guest lecture, the 25 model letters, Heidi Roizen's rules, and live class discussion
This appendix consolidates every cold outreach lesson from Winning Writing into one reference. It combines the formal ten rules with the in-class commentary from Rachel, Glenn, Heidi, and the live student critiques. The goal is a checklist you can use the night before you send a cold email to a CEO, a VC, a hiring manager, or anyone who has more power than you.

# The philosophy
A cold email is a pitch. Not a request, not an introduction, not a summary of your resume. It is a pitch in which you are selling something: yourself, your company, your idea. The JFK principle applies. Don't pitch yourself. Pitch what you can do for them. As Rachel put it: tell me how you're going to make my life astronomically better than before I met you.

The person you're emailing gets dozens of cold emails a week if they're a senior exec or VC. You have about fifteen seconds. Everything in this playbook exists to survive those fifteen seconds.

# The ten rules (enriched with class commentary)
## 1. Know something about the person and exploit it
Research the person extensively before writing a single word. LinkedIn, podcasts, blog posts, recent news, their portfolio if they're a VC. People drop incredibly personal details in twenty-minute podcast interviews. A Google News search is non-negotiable. The goal is to know enough that when they read your email, they think: this person did their homework.
From the model letters: Amadeus referenced swimming from Hog Heaven toward Necker Island. Mike Kearnes referenced jumping into Lake Lanier with McMaster in 2014. Pauline found Davis Guggenheim in Rwanda and used that as a bridge to Thomas Newman. The specificity of the research is what separates a real email from a template.

## 2. Begin with something they do not know
Don't open with flattery about their accomplishments. They know what they've done. Tell me something I don't know is the cardinal rule. The bad Bezos letter opens with "You have transformed industry after industry." The good version opens with "I can help Alexa help patients needing personalized follow-up care."
Rachel's version: tell me a secret about the future. If you can articulate an insight about where your target's industry is going that they haven't heard before, they will respond. It doesn't matter if you're slightly wrong. They want to know that you have a dazzling, unique thesis they've never heard and you expressed it in one sentence.

## 3. Write a subject line that cannot be ignored
The subject line determines whether the email gets opened. Personal, timely, unusual. Not "Thank you." Not "Hoping to connect." Not "Quick question."
Examples that work from class: "I'm wearing that hideous bracelet you gave me in 2011." "The long walk from a small, brown village to Stanford." "Stanford GSB, I am raising a search fund." "Coffee chat with Stanford MBA who manages a boxing World Champion." "Stanford MBA who wants to build agents with you."
Rachel's addition: for job-seeking emails, consider leading with your most distinctive credential in the subject. "Ex Olympic trials swimmer" or "Bush pilot turned GSB student" will get opened. "Stanford MBA seeking PM role" will not.

## 4. Find a mutual contact and name them
"X suggested I reach out." The bar is higher than a LinkedIn connection. It needs to be someone you have actually engaged with. The student who wrote to Himad Zahed opened with "Paul Quan encouraged me to introduce myself" and it immediately established that this wasn't a random cold email.
Heidi's addition: if someone agrees to forward your email, don't send one email and say "please send to A, B, and C." That forces them to create three separate emails and remove other names. Instead, send three individually tailored emails so all they have to do is forward each one.

## 5. Compare yourself to the recipient
The "like you" move creates an instant connection. "Like you, I grew up eating Korean food lovingly prepared by a single mom." "Like you, I spent years pipetting and cloning before deciding the most interesting problem sits where science meets business."
Be careful with this. "Like you, I am super smart and super successful" is boastful and will backfire. "Like you, I believe AI will transform the future" is meaningless because everyone believes that. The comparison needs to be specific and genuine. And never use the diminishing version: "Like you, but at a vastly smaller scale." They already know that.
Heidi's note: some people email her saying all their classmates call them the Heidi Roizen of their class. That line works, but only with a bit of humor.

## 6. Tell a story that makes them feel something
The letters that get responses are the ones with a scene, not a summary. Pauline listening to Thomas Newman in her Harvard dorm room. Amadeus training for an Ironman after staring at Necker Island. The punk rocker sitting on stage with Fugazi at the Excelsior Mill in 1989 wearing his dad's ill-fitting combat boots with egg in his hair. Alex Zeng's letter to a Walmart warehouse manager: "You have 12 years at Walmart. I have three weeks at Dexterity, a month-old startup with fewer than 10 employees. And that is why I'm reaching out."
Make it multisensory. A date, a location, a line of dialogue, an image. The more specific the scene, the harder it is to ignore.

## 7. Pick a lane
Do not cover everything on your resume. Give one example of your talent that shows how you can help that specific person. Link to LinkedIn in the signature for the full background. Alex Menke's letter to Lynn Jurich at Sunrun uses four crisp bullet points covering his background, then spends the rest of the email on the offer to help build Sunrun's home energy management service.
Rachel's version for consultants specifically: the stigma against consultants in tech and startups is real. Elon, Vinod, Mark Andreessen all say they don't hire consultants. The perception is that consultants "empower, enable, and strategize" but don't get things done. In your email, talk about projects you took from concept through execution. "This is what it looked like on the day of launch, and I did it."

## 8. Be confident but humble
Heidi's rule: easier said than done. If in doubt, have a friend read it first. The sweet spot is what Adam Bryant calls "desirable confidence." The student who wrote to Condoleezza Rice pulled this off. TP Katene's letter to Kesha Cash is confident about his mission but humble about what he doesn't yet know. Bank's letter to Chatri Sityodtong acknowledges "I was fortunate enough not to have to live on $4 a day while doing my MBA" while still making a bold ask.

## 9. Keep it short with a small, easy ask
Long emails don't get answered because they take too much time to read and the ask gets buried. Heidi's critical advice: make your ask as small as possible to meet your needs. Not "coffee for an hour to give me career advice." Instead: "ten minutes because I'm choosing between a PM role at Google and director of products at a twenty-person startup, I've fleshed out my pros and cons, and I'd love your take on the choice."
Make the ask as easy to act on as possible. Read it through from the recipient's eyes and eliminate any step you've created for them that you could do yourself.
Leave the door open for no. "I know that's a big ask, so no is a perfectly fine answer." This is appreciated more than people realize.
Rachel's addition: never say "if you ever find yourself near Stanford." If this person says "fly to Austin tomorrow," you will be there. Say "I will come to you, anywhere, anytime." And never suggest "coffee." Suggest something specific to them. If they're a cyclist, suggest a ride. If they're a swimmer, reference that. The specificity shows you've done the research.

## 10. Offer something in return
The single best way to quintuple response rates from CEOs is to stop asking them for things and help them scale their reputation. Invite them to speak at a class, a podcast, an event. CEOs manage their time twelve to eighteen months out. A lunch request gets ignored. An invitation to speak at the world's top business school gets answered. Then you moderate the session, meet them in the green room, and have done something genuinely valuable for them.
From the model letters: Kirin Sinha offered to share her findings on game design in AR. Adriano Piccinin offered to share stories about struggles and mistakes in Brazil. Murat offered an invitation to the first GSB Search Fund Conference. The offer doesn't have to be enormous. It just has to show you're not only taking.

# Lines that kill cold emails
"I hope you are well." The most generic opening in existence. Signals you have nothing interesting to say.
"My name is [X]." It's in the email header.
"Good morning" or "good afternoon." You don't know their time zone.
"I'd love to pick your brain." CEOs don't have time. Offer something instead.
"I'd love to grab coffee." Say: "I'll come to your office anytime." Or suggest an activity they actually enjoy.
"I'd be grateful for the chance to learn more about where someone of my background could help." Too vague. Tell them specifically how you can help.
Sending to jobs@company.com or pitches@firm.com. Find the actual person. Guess the email format, BCC all variations, use tools like Rocket Reach.
"Sheryl Samberg" or "Mark Andreesen at Sequoia." Check names, firm names, and titles twice. Wrong details destroy credibility instantly.
"I think" when you actually know. "I think the forecast needs revising" versus "We need to lower Q3 sales forecast by 50%."
"Like you, but at a vastly smaller scale." They already know that.

# The follow-up and the AB test
The class ran a real-time AB test. The exact same email sent to jobs@anthropic.com received zero response. The identical email sent directly to the head of executive recruiting at Anthropic received a response at 8:25 AM the next morning, thirteen hours later.

If your first email gets read but not responded to, do not reply with "just following up." Write a new email that references the first one without being needy. Maintain or escalate the ask. Show that you haven't backed down. The new email should have new information, a new hook, or a new reason for urgency.

# Rachel's additions from the live lecture
## Tell me a secret about the future
The best pitches, whether for a job or a company, contain an insight about the future that the recipient hasn't heard before. The student writing to TML about bush piloting in Kenya had great personal material but didn't go far enough. He needed to tell the recipient something new and dazzling about how he thinks, then connect it to the company's thesis. Even if you're slightly in disagreement with something they've said, that's fine. Nobody will refuse to hire you because they slightly disagree with a thesis. They want to know you can articulate your own epistemology.

## The S.H.I.T. framework for pitching yourself
Simple: especially if your background is complex, simplify it. Heroic: position yourself as the protagonist. Will this person prevail? Stay tuned. Inevitable: whatever you're pursuing is on the right side of history. Timely: you're doing this now for a reason.
This framework applies to personal positioning, not just companies. Your LinkedIn bio is your mission statement. Everyone in this room has a "what matters to you most and why" essay that got them in. Use that as a starting point.

## Don't let the bots brand you
If you don't define your brand, someone else will, and it won't be flattering. Rachel's wall of shame from her class: four people said "I'm left brain and right brain," eight said "I build and scale to maximize impact," and the winner was "I'm drawn to complex problems." All of these take what's unique and special about a person and turn it into generic slop.
Other phrases to eliminate from all professional writing: "intersection of strategy, product, and execution" (the most expensive intersection in Silicon Valley is a cliche), "driving innovation," "building and scaling," "passionate about complex problems." These are all words that ChatGPT produces and that every hiring manager, VC, and journalist has learned to skip.

## Consultants: how to overcome the stigma
Rachel's specific advice for anyone coming from consulting: the perception in tech and startups is that consultants are "PowerPoint jockeys" who empower, enable, and strategize but don't execute. In every email, interview, and LinkedIn bio, talk about projects you took from concept through execution. "This is what it looked like on launch day, and I did it." The word "drive" is banned. You drive cars, not synergies. The words "strategize," "empower," and "enable" are toxic because they confirm the consultant stereotype.

# Patterns from the live student critiques
Several cold emails were workshopped in class. The feedback patterns repeated:

### The Green Beret at General Catalyst (the model)
This email did almost everything right. It named a mutual contact up front (Paul Quan). It used the "like you" move with humor ("collecting degrees like infinity stones"). It told a specific personal story (Florida to Alaska at 5, Green Beret for nine years). Most importantly, it explained how the sender could help the firm: "GCI needs a permanent investment presence in DC. I'm the person to build it." Glenn's one correction: make sure the recipient knows you're already at GC, since that context changes everything.

### The TML letter (the fix)
Good personal material (bush pilot in Kenya, first edition book). The fix: don't tell the recipient what their own role is. Tell them something they don't know. Lead with the book as a gift. Replace the intellectual curiosity section with a specific thesis about the future that connects the bush pilot experience to what TML needs. Rachel: "In an era of AI when you can get all sorts of synthesized knowledge, you actually need someone who can firefight ambiguous and challenging situations. That's the perfect experience at an AI company."

### The Lance Armstrong at Next Ventures letter (the tighten)
Great PS about Olympic swim trials and cycling. The fix: move the athlete identity to the top or the subject line, because that's what gets this email opened at a firm where every partner is a former athlete. Cut the middle paragraph that agrees with what he already knows. Replace with a specific thesis about where to invest in the attention/health space. Rachel: "Tell me which niche to invest in. He wants to know you have a dazzling thesis he's never heard before, expressed in one sentence."

### The biodiversity startup letter (the reframe)
Good research background, good "like you" comparison (both lab scientists turned business). The fix: be honest about whether you're competing or collaborating. If your business model resembles theirs, say so. Don't mislead. And shift from intellectual curiosity ("I'd like to understand why") to a concrete offer of how you can help.

# Pre-send checklist for cold outreach
Subject line: would this person actually open this? Is it personal, timely, and unusual?
First sentence: does it tell them something they don't know, or does it tell them what they already know about themselves?
Like you: is there a genuine, specific comparison? Not generic ("we both believe in AI").
Story: is there one vivid scene with sensory detail? A date, a place, a line of dialogue?
How you help them: have you explained specifically what you can do for them, not just what you want from them?
Secret about the future: do you have one insight or thesis they haven't heard before?
Ask: is it small, specific, and easy to say yes to? Have you left the door open for no?
Offer: are you giving something, not just taking?
Length: under 200 words?
Jargon: leverage, align, synergize, drive, impact, strategize, empower, enable? All cut?
Names and details: checked twice? Right firm, right spelling, right title?
Read aloud: does it sound like you talking, or like a ChatGPT template? If two bold words followed by a sentence in bullets, rewrite.
Sent to the right person: not jobs@company.com. The actual human. Email guessed, BCC'd, Rocket Reach'd.
Sign-off: something with personality, not "Best" or "Sincerely."
